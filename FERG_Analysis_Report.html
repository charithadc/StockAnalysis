01 — Overview

Executive Summary

Ferguson Enterprises is North America's largest distributor of plumbing, HVAC, and waterworks products — a scale-advantaged, essential-infrastructure business serving professional contractors across 1,700+ locations in the US and Canada.

This report forecasts Revenue per Share, EPS, and P/E ranges for CY2026–CY2029, grounded in analyst consensus, recent earnings, and Ferguson's structural growth drivers: non-residential construction, data center buildout, tuck-in acquisitions, and a newly-aligned calendar fiscal year.

Note: Ferguson changed its fiscal year end from July 31 to December 31. CY2025 is now the first full calendar year under the new structure. CY2026 commenced January 1, 2026. All forecasts use the new calendar year alignment.

03 — Business

Three Core End Markets

  • Non-Residential (~50% of US revenue) — The primary growth engine. Grew 12% YoY in Q1 CY2026. Key drivers include data center construction, infrastructure, industrial, and commercial projects. Ferguson's large capital project TAM has been upgraded to $90B. Data center proximity is a key competitive advantage cited by Bernstein and Jefferies.
  • Residential (~50% of US revenue) — A near-term headwind but long-term tailwind. Declining 1% YoY in CY2026 due to weak housing starts and HVAC replacement slowdown. Recovery tied to rate cuts and housing activity — a meaningful re-acceleration could materially exceed current forecasts.
  • Canada (~3–4% of revenue) — Modest contributor. Sales grew 2.2% in Q1 CY2026 but operating profit declined 30% — reflecting margin pressure. Not a key driver for the investment thesis, but stable.

Ferguson's business model is distribution-scale with high customer stickiness. Gross margins held at 30.7% in Q1 CY2026, up 60bps YoY — demonstrating pricing discipline despite mixed revenue growth. Operating margin guidance for CY2026 is 9.4–9.8%.

Why P/E May Expand or Contract

  • Bear case (19–22x): Rates stay high, residential market stagnates through 2027, data center cycle peaks early. EPS growth remains low-single-digit; multiple compresses toward distributor-sector floor.
  • Base case (23–25x): Current levels sustained. Gradual residential recovery, steady non-residential growth, consistent mid-to-high single-digit EPS. The most likely outcome given guidance and analyst consensus.
  • Bull case (26–28x): Rate cuts restore housing demand, Copilot-style digital tools boost distributor margins, large capital projects (data centres) sustain 10%+ non-res growth for 3+ years. Re-rating toward the top of Ferguson's historical range.

06 — Catalysts

Key Bull Case Drivers

  • Data center supercycle: Bernstein raised its price target to $310 citing Ferguson's proximity to upcoming data center construction sites. The large capital project TAM has been upgraded to $90B. Ferguson's scale and multi-product "One Ferguson" model wins disproportionate share on complex projects.
  • Residential recovery: If the Fed cuts rates meaningfully, housing starts, HVAC replacement, and residential RMI — together ~50% of US net sales — return to growth. Even a return to flat would remove a meaningful headwind currently suppressing EPS.
  • Acquisition engine: Ferguson invested $276M in eight acquisitions in CY2025 alone, generating $300M+ in annualised revenue. The market is deeply fragmented; the pipeline is extensive. Deals typically close at 6–8x EBITDA and are immediately accretive.
  • Share buyback machine: $0.9B repurchased in CY2025, ~$800M program balance remaining plus a new $5B authorisation underway. At current prices, buybacks reduce the share count by ~3–4% annually, mechanically lifting EPS even with flat earnings.
  • Operating leverage: Gross margin expanded 60bps to 30.7% in Q1 CY2026. As the revenue mix shifts toward higher-margin private label, digital channel sales, and large project wins, operating margins can expand from ~9.6% toward 10%+ without top-line heroics.

07 — Risks

Key Risk Factors

HIGH
Prolonged Residential Weakness Residential is ~50% of US net sales and remains down ~1% YoY. High mortgage rates continue to suppress new housing starts and HVAC replacement demand. A "higher for longer" rate environment could delay recovery past 2027, materially compressing EPS growth versus this forecast.
HIGH
Macro / Tariff Shock A US recession or escalating trade tariffs could cause contractors to defer large capital projects and cut discretionary spending. Ferguson's 2026 earnings call cited tariff uncertainty as an active watch item. Input cost inflation (currently ~3%) could also squeeze margins if demand weakens and pricing power fades.
MEDIUM
Competitive Pressure While the market is fragmented, regional distributors and national players (Watsco, Wesco, HD Supply) are also consolidating aggressively. Amazon Business and digital procurement platforms represent a longer-term structural challenge to the traditional distribution model.
MEDIUM
HVAC Refrigerant Transition Headwind The regulatory shift to A2L refrigerants (away from R-410A) created a pull-forward of demand in 2024 followed by a hangover in early CY2026. The comparison period is tough and HVAC revenue declined 1% YoY in Q1 CY2026. This normalises by mid-2026 but creates near-term noise.
LOW
Canada Margin Pressure Canadian adjusted operating profit fell 30.4% in Q1 CY2026, a sharp reversal. Canada is a small contributor (~3–4% of revenue) but highlights execution risk in non-US markets. Monitoring warranted but not thesis-critical.
LOW
Fiscal Year Transition Noise The five-month transition period (August–December 2025) complicates historical year-over-year comparisons. Analysts and investors are still recalibrating to the new calendar year structure. This creates short-term sentiment risk but no fundamental impact.

Overall assessment: FERG carries low fundamental business risk — essential products, sticky customers, mission-critical projects. Primary near-term risk is macro/rate-driven demand weakness, not competitive displacement. The balance sheet is conservative with net debt/EBITDA of just 1.1x, providing ample M&A and buyback firepower.

08 — Conclusion

Investment Conclusion

Ferguson is a high-quality, durable compounder with a dominant market position in a structurally growing, fragmented industry. Despite near-term headwinds from residential weakness and the fiscal year transition, the non-residential growth engine is firing, margins are expanding, and the buyback engine continues to work.
