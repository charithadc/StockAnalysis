02 — Thesis

Executive Summary

Core thesis: Lam Research sits at the structural intersection of two compounding tailwinds — rising etch and deposition intensity per wafer driven by AI-era 3D architectures, and a multi-year wafer fabrication equipment (WFE) capex supercycle now visible into 2028. The fundamental business has rarely looked stronger: record revenue, record margins, ROIC near 45%, and 85%+ of free cash flow returned to shareholders. The catch is valuation. After a 200%+ rally in twelve months, LRCX trades at roughly 48× trailing earnings versus a 22× ten-year average — meaning the next four years require Lam to grow into the multiple, not for the multiple to do the lifting.

This report works through a deliberately conservative model: revenue compounding ~13% annually through fiscal 2029, EPS reaching roughly $9.95, and the multiple gradually mean-reverting toward the historical band. Under that base case, the stock returns roughly 0–5% annualised over four years from today's $272. The bull case (sustained 30× multiple, faster NAND conversion, China headwinds offset by elsewhere) gets you to ~$320. The bear case — a cyclical pause and re-rating to 18–20× — implies meaningful downside. This is a fundamentally great business priced for a fundamentally great future.

04 — Moat

Business Model & Moat

Lam Research designs and sells the deposition and etch tools that build and pattern the layers of every advanced logic and memory chip. The business splits into two engines: Systems (~64% of revenue) — the high-ticket equipment shipped to fabs — and the Customer Support Business Group / CSBG (~36%) — services, spares, upgrades, and software running on an installed base that now exceeds 100,000 chambers. CSBG is the quiet compounder: it grows with the installed base, carries higher margin, and dampens the cyclicality that Wall Street historically priced into the systems business.

Why customers can't easily switch

  • Process qualification lock-in. Once a fab qualifies a Lam tool for a process step at a given node, swapping to a competitor requires months of re-qualification and yield risk that customers almost never accept mid-cycle.
  • #1 share in etch, #2 in deposition. Etch is the single largest WFE category; in conductor etch and select dielectric etch applications Lam holds dominant share. Recent dielectric-etch wins at a leading foundry expand the footprint into territory long held by competitors.
  • AI-driven intensity tailwind. 3D NAND layer counts, gate-all-around transistors, backside power delivery, and high-bandwidth memory all add etch and deposition steps per wafer — a structural per-wafer dollar uplift for Lam regardless of WFE direction.
  • Capital-light, high ROIC model. Capex runs at 4–5% of revenue. Free cash flow conversion is exceptional, enabling 139% of free cash flow returned to shareholders in the March quarter alone.
  • Advanced packaging optionality. Lam's copper plating and through-silicon-via etch tools are core to HBM and chiplet architectures, an area management sees growing 50%+ in calendar 2026.

14 — Pros & Cons

Structural Advantages & Key Risks

Structural Advantages

  • #1 share in etch and #2 in deposition — both irreplaceable in any advanced fab
  • ~45% ROIC with 4–5% capex intensity — exceptional capital efficiency
  • 100,000+ chamber installed base anchors recurring CSBG revenue
  • AI-era step-change in etch/deposition intensity per wafer
  • 50%+ gross margin, durable per management commentary
  • $5.4B+ free cash flow, 85%+ returned to shareholders
  • Recent dielectric-etch wins at a major foundry expand TAM
  • Advanced packaging revenue growing 50%+ in CY26

Key Risks

  • Trailing P/E at 48–55× vs. 22× 10-year average
  • ~43% of revenue from China — declining to <30% in FY26
  • Cyclical industry — memory pause or foundry digestion possible
  • U.S. export controls could tighten further
  • Beta of 1.79 — significant drawdown risk in tech selloffs
  • Customer concentration: TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, Intel
  • Insider selling activity from CFO during March
  • WFE could plateau in 2028 once NAND conversion completes

Risk Severity Assessment

HIGH
Valuation reset risk. Trailing P/E sits more than 100% above its 10-year average. A pause in industry capex narrative — or even a normal cycle digestion — could compress the multiple to 25–30× and send the stock into a 25–35% drawdown despite stable earnings.
HIGH
China revenue compression. Roughly 43% of fiscal 2026 revenue came from China; the 50% affiliate rule and incremental export controls have already created a $200M Q2 drag and are guided to a ~$600M calendar-2026 headwind. Further controls cannot be ruled out.
MED
Customer concentration. Five customers (TSMC, Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, Intel) likely account for over 60% of revenue. A capex pause from any one of them is material; a coordinated pause would be severe.
MED
Memory cycle exposure. Memory remains 39% of systems. Historical memory downturns have compressed Lam's revenue by 25–35%. The next downturn — whenever it comes — will be brutal even if structurally shallower than past cycles.
LOW
Competitive displacement. Tokyo Electron, Applied Materials, and ASM International compete in adjacent areas, but qualification lock-in and Lam's installed-base support make share losses slow-moving and recoverable.
LOW
Execution / management quality. Tim Archer (CEO) and Doug Bettinger (CFO) have a multi-year track record of conservative guidance and consistent beat-and-raise. Management bench is deep.
15 — Macro & Competition

Macro, Regulation & Competition

Macro sensitivities

  • Interest rates. Lam is a long-duration earnings stock (50%+ ROIC, growing earnings stream). Higher-for-longer rates compress justifiable multiples; rate cuts would relieve valuation pressure.
  • FX. ~80% of revenue is non-US, but most invoicing is in USD, so direct FX exposure is moderate. Indirect exposure runs through customer capex willingness in Korea, Japan, Taiwan.
  • Recession sensitivity. Equipment orders are leading indicators of consumer electronics demand. A consumer recession typically hits memory pricing first, then 6–12 months later WFE orders. Lam's recovering NAND segment is the most cyclically vulnerable component.
  • AI capex sustainability. The single most important macro variable. If hyperscaler capex normalises rather than continues compounding, advanced-node demand and HBM intensity both moderate, and Lam's bull case unwinds.
  • Inflation in components. Lam has notified suppliers to source non-Chinese alternatives for certain components, which could raise input costs.

Regulatory & geopolitical

Regulatory assumption baked into base case: the December 2024 export-control package is fully reflected, and a meaningful incremental tightening occurs late in calendar 2026 or early 2027. China revenue normalises near 25% of total by FY28. A sharper regulatory regime — including outright bans on a wider class of mature-node equipment to China — would push my Bear case toward the downside scenarios.
  • December 2024 export controls already cost Lam an estimated $200M in revenue with ongoing impact
  • 50% affiliate rule restricts shipments to specific Chinese customers, forcing China revenue below 30% in FY26
  • Supply-chain re-routing away from Chinese components is underway and may compress margins modestly
  • U.S. CHIPS Act incentives for domestic fab build-out (TSMC Arizona, Samsung Texas, Intel Ohio) are a structural offset

Competitive landscape

  • Applied Materials (AMAT). Largest WFE company by revenue, broader product portfolio across deposition, etch, and inspection. Lam has narrower scope but deeper share in its specific niches. AMAT trades at ~40× P/E — also rich, but less stretched than LRCX.
  • Tokyo Electron (TEL). Direct competitor in etch, deposition, and coater/developer. Holds ~9–12% of WFE share. May benefit from being exempted from some U.S. export controls — a relative competitive risk for Lam.
  • KLA Corporation (KLAC). Process control and metrology leader; complementary rather than directly competitive in most cases. Trades at ~50× P/E — a similar valuation profile and useful comp.
  • ASML. Lithography monopoly, complementary not competitive. Useful comp for valuation purposes but a different business model.
  • ASM International, Hitachi High-Tech, Screen Holdings. Smaller specialists in deposition, etch, and clean — niche competitors with limited share threat.
16 — Conclusion

Investment Conclusion

The Honest Verdict

Lam Research is one of the highest-quality industrials in the world. The thesis isn't broken — it's already paid out. The 200%+ rally over the past twelve months has effectively pulled forward four years of base-case returns into a single year. Buying here is a bet that the multiple holds at peak levels and that the cycle extends without pause through 2028. Possible — but not the way I'd handicap it.
