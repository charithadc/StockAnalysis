02 — Executive Summary

The Best Independent Wealth Platform, Mispriced on AI Headlines

Thesis. LPLA is the dominant scale player in US independent wealth management, with $2.4T in assets, 32,000+ advisors, an 8% organic NNA growth rate, and a track record of compounding adjusted EPS at ~25% over five years. The February 2026 AI-disruption selloff has created the cheapest entry multiple LPLA has traded at in over a decade — ~16x forward earnings — at exactly the moment Commonwealth integration is set to inflect run-rate EBITDA by ~$415M+.

Setup. Stock is down 20% from its 52-week high of $403, with the AI narrative providing the cover for valuation reset rather than the cause of business deterioration. FY2025 adjusted EPS hit $20.09 (+22%), Q4 was a beat, and 14 of 14 sell-side analysts rate the stock Buy or Strong Buy at an average target of $404 (+26%). The base case offers an attractive risk/reward; the bear case requires a structural break in advisor economics that has not yet shown up in retention (97%) or recruiting ($104B in 2025).

Key insight on numbers: All EPS figures in this report use adjusted (non-GAAP) earnings, which is the metric the Street and management use for guidance. GAAP EPS in 2025 was $10.92 (down 22% YoY), distorted by ~$420M in Commonwealth purchase-accounting acquisition costs. Adjusted EPS of $20.09 is the more meaningful comparison for forward modeling.

04 — Business Model & Moat

The Open-Architecture Toll Road of US Wealth

LPL is not a wirehouse and not a robo. It is the infrastructure for ~32,000 independent advisors who keep their own books and pay LPL for clearing, custody, technology, compliance, and platform services. The economics are a layered toll: a commission/advisory payout on flowing assets (advisors keep ~88% of gross revenue), asset-based fees on advisory accounts, service fees on the platform, and — most importantly today — a fat slice of net interest income on $61B of client cash held in FDIC sweeps. The cash-sorting cycle hurts when rates fall; the rest of the model is structurally compounding.

The moat is composed of switching costs (advisor practices are physically and operationally embedded in LPL infrastructure for years at a time), scale (LPL spreads tech and compliance investment across more advisors than anyone else), and a unique multi-channel affiliation suite — pure-RIA, hybrid, employee, institution-services — that lets LPL absorb advisors from any origin point. Retention sits at 97%, recruited assets hit a record $104B in 2025, and the platform onboarded Atria, Prudential, Wintrust, First Horizon, and Commonwealth in a single 18-month stretch.

  • 🔒Switching costs. Once an advisor's book is on LPL's clearing and tech stack, leaving means re-papering thousands of client accounts. 97% asset retention proves stickiness.
  • 📈Scale flywheel. Largest US independent BD by a wide margin. Tech and compliance spend per advisor falls as the base grows; competitors can't match unit economics.
  • 🏦Cash franchise. $61B of client cash sweeps generated $456M of revenue in Q4 alone, with a 341 bps yield. A natural hedge: when markets fall, cash balances rise.
  • 🔁Capital allocation. Disciplined M&A integrator (Atria, Prudential, Commonwealth, Investment Center). Buybacks paused only to delever post-Commonwealth — a feature, not a bug.
  • 🏢Institution Services. Increasingly running back-office for major banks and insurers (Prudential, First Horizon, Wintrust). High-margin, sticky B2B revenue stream most of the Street still under-models.
05 — Revenue Sources & Segments

Where the $16.6B Comes From

FY2025 net revenue mix is dominated by advisory fees and commission revenue, which together represent ~75% of the top line and scale directly with advisory and brokerage assets. Asset-based fees (the line that includes client-cash sweep revenue) is the most rate-sensitive and the single most-watched item on LPL's P&L. Service & fee, plus transaction revenue, are platform tolls that scale with advisor count.

Advisory Revenue~50%
Commission Revenue~25%
Asset-based Fees (Cash Sweep)~16%
Service & Fee Revenue~4%

The investment debate is largely about the 16% line. Asset-based fees are net interest income — sensitive to short-rate trajectory and to how much client cash sits idle on the platform. With the Fed in a cutting cycle, the consensus has already baked in some yield compression on the ~$60B sweep balance. My model assumes a 30-bps yield decline through 2027 before stabilization, partially offset by ~$8–10B of growth in cash balances as Commonwealth assets convert.

10 — P/E Multiple Forecast

The Multiple is the Crux of the Trade

LPLA's 5-year average P/E sits at ~24x (10-year average ~20x). Today the stock trades at ~16x my 2026 EPS and ~12x 2027 — a 4-to-8-turn discount to its own history. The market is pricing in either (a) a permanent fee-compression regime from AI tools, (b) a structural decline in cash-sweep yields, or (c) some combination. My base case assumes a partial multiple recovery — to 17–18x — once the AI narrative cools and Commonwealth synergies are visible in the numbers.

14 — Structural Advantages & Key Risks

What Helps. What Hurts.

✓ Structural Advantages

  • Largest US independent BD by 3x. Scale flywheel in tech and compliance.
  • 97% advisor retention. Best-in-class stickiness.
  • $104B in 2025 recruiting. Wirehouse and bank-channel breakaways still flowing.
  • Multi-channel platform. Pure-RIA, hybrid, employee, institution. Captures all advisor flows.
  • Disciplined integrator. Atria, Prudential, Commonwealth all on track.
  • Capital-light. Recurring fees + sweep NIM, low capex, high FCF conversion.

✗ Key Risks

  • AI fee compression. Tools like Altruist's Hazel could erode advisory pricing power.
  • Cash-sweep yield decline. Fed cuts + sweep-rate regulation pressure NII.
  • Commonwealth retention miss. Below-90% retention destroys deal IRR.
  • Regulatory overhang. SEC AML penalty in 2024; Reg-BI standards still evolving.
  • Leverage post-deal. 2.25x at close; deleveraging path requires no surprises.
  • Equity-market beta. ~50% of revenue scales with asset levels.

15 — Macro, Regulation & Competition

The External Environment

Macro Factors

  • 📉Interest rates (HIGH sensitivity). ~16% of revenue is net interest income on $61B of client cash. Each 25 bps Fed cut translates to roughly $40–60M of annualized revenue decline, partially offset by higher cash balances during equity drawdowns. Current trajectory: 50–75 bps of further cuts in 2026, then stabilization.
  • 📈Equity-market levels (HIGH sensitivity). Advisory fees are a percentage of AUM; a 10% S&P drop directly hits ~50% of LPL's revenue mix. Beta of 0.87 understates the underlying business sensitivity.
  • 💱FX (LOW). Effectively zero — LPL is 100% US-domestic.
  • 💼Recession sensitivity (MEDIUM). A recession would slow recruiting and dampen NNA, but the recurring-revenue base (advisory fees + service fees) provides a meaningful floor. 2008-style trauma is a tail risk; mild recessions are absorbable.
  • 📊Inflation (LOW). Asset levels generally rise with inflation; wage cost inflation is real but spread across 10K+ employees and offset by tech leverage.

Regulation

Regulatory assumptions in my model: No structural change to FDIC sweep rules in the forecast window. SEC Reg-BI compliance costs continue at 2024–25 run-rate. No major DOL fiduciary rule reset. If any of these break adversely, expect 100–200 bps of margin pressure — material but not thesis-breaking. The 2024 $18M SEC AML penalty is in the rearview.

Competition

The competitive set is real but LPL's positioning is unique within it.

  • 🏛Charles Schwab (SCHW). 7x larger by assets but a custodian first, advisor platform second. Different revenue mix (more retail, more securities lending). Direct overlap mainly in RIA-custody segment.
  • 🏦Raymond James (RJF). Most direct competitor in independent advisor channel. Similar size in advisor count; LPL leads on technology and onboarding speed.
  • 🏢Stifel, Ameriprise, Wells Fargo Advisors. Scale BD competitors. LPL's moat is independent-channel positioning that wirehouses can't replicate without cannibalizing employee-advisor businesses.
  • ⚙️Altruist (private). The new disruptor. Hazel AI tax tool sparked the February 2026 selloff. LPL's response is happening — its Liquidity & Succession program and Linsco channel address the same advisor-economics pain point that Altruist targets.
  • 🔧Fidelity, Pershing/BNY. Custody competitors. Important for RIA-custody segment but not for the core BD business that drives 75% of LPL revenue.
16 — Investment Conclusion

The Bottom Line

LPLA is the highest-quality scaled platform in US independent wealth management, trading at a ~30% discount to its 5-year average multiple because the market is repricing for AI fee compression that has not yet shown up in retention, recruiting, or pricing data. The Commonwealth integration is the single largest near-term catalyst — done well, it adds ~$425M to run-rate EBITDA. Done poorly, it adds leverage and integration drag at exactly the wrong time. Eight consecutive quarterly EPS beats, a 14-of-14 Buy/Strong-Buy consensus, and a forward P/E in the low teens point to an asymmetric setup.
