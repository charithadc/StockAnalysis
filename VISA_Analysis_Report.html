01 — Overview

Executive Summary

Visa is the world's dominant payment network — processing $17 trillion in card payments annually while operating at ~67% operating margins. Yet as of April 2026, the stock trades at a meaningful discount to its own 5-year average valuation, compressed by regulatory fears that are being slowly, steadily resolved.

This report forecasts Revenue per Share, EPS, and P/E ranges for FY2026–FY2029, anchored in Q1 FY2026 earnings, analyst consensus, and Visa's strategic pivot from a card network to a "network of networks" spanning B2B, Visa Direct, and AI-powered commerce.

Note: Visa's fiscal year ends September 30. FY2025 = Oct 2024–Sep 2025. FY2026 = Oct 2025–Sep 2026. EPS figures reference non-GAAP (adjusted) unless otherwise stated.

03 — Business Model

The Network of Networks

  • Consumer Payments (~55% of revenue) — The core toll-booth. Every swipe, tap, and click on Visa-branded cards earns a small percentage fee. Payments volume grew +8% YoY on constant currency in FY2025. Cross-border volume (the highest-margin category) grew +13% constant currency.
  • New Flows — B2B & Visa Direct (~20% of revenue) — Visa's largest future opportunity. The B2B payments market is a $120 trillion annual market still dominated by paper checks. Visa Direct processed 12.5 billion transactions in 2025, up sharply YoY, reaching 12 billion endpoints globally across cards, accounts, and wallets.
  • Value-Added Services (~25% of revenue) — Fraud prevention, consulting, tokenization, and data analytics layered on top of the network. VAS and "New Flows" now represent ~45% of total revenue, diversifying away from pure interchange dependency. Over 10 billion tokens issued by end of 2025.

Revenue mix is actively diversifying toward stickier, higher-margin services less exposed to interchange fee compression — the structural bear case for Visa. The strategic pivot to "network of networks" and AI-driven agentic commerce is early-stage but accelerating.

What P/E Re-Rating Looks Like

  • Bear case (22–24x): CCCA passes in strong form, materially compressing interchange fees. Consumer spending slowdown driven by recession. Cross-border travel softens. EPS growth drops to low single digits.
  • Base case (26–28x): Current valuation range sustained or gradually recovered. Steady 11–14% EPS growth, regulatory risk stays unresolved but manageable. Visa Direct and VAS offset any consumer slowdown. Most likely outcome given the wide analyst consensus.
  • Bull case (30–34x): Return toward 5-year historical average. Achievable if CCCA fails, AI agentic commerce accelerates revenue surprise upward, and rate cuts re-rate tech/growth multiples broadly. At 32x on $20 EPS, the stock would trade at ~$640.

06 — Catalysts

Key Bull Case Drivers

  • Network effects compound forever: Visa's network becomes more valuable with every incremental merchant and cardholder. With 61% market share in digital payments globally, this moat grows wider every year — not narrower.
  • B2B — the $120T frontier: Visa has identified $35T in B2B commercial payments that can migrate to its rails. Visa Direct already reaches 12B endpoints. Even modest penetration of B2B flows is a multi-decade revenue growth engine.
  • AI Agentic Commerce: Visa Intelligent Commerce, launched in 2025, enables AI agents to make purchases within pre-approved parameters. As AI assistants proliferate, Visa's global rail becomes the default settlement layer for machine-initiated transactions — a truly novel, high-margin traffic type.
  • FIFA World Cup 2026 tailwind: As an exclusive worldwide payments partner for the 2026 World Cup (hosted in North America), Visa benefits from elevated cross-border spend, brand exposure, and transaction volume in the highest-margin category during FY2026–27.
  • Share buyback machine: Visa returned $5.1B to shareholders in Q1 FY2026 alone through buybacks and dividends, reducing share count ~3% per year. On a $30B authorized buyback program, this mechanical EPS lift continues for years regardless of revenue conditions.
  • Valuation discount is anomalous: At ~24x forward EPS, Visa trades 30% below its own 5-year P/E average. This discount reflects regulatory fear, not fundamental deterioration. As regulatory clarity improves, re-rating alone would push the stock to $390–420.

07 — Risks

Key Risk Factors

HIGH
Credit Card Competition Act (CCCA) Reintroduced in January 2026, this legislation would require large banks to offer a second routing network other than Visa or Mastercard. If passed in its strong form, it could trigger a "race to the bottom" on interchange fees — the primary bear case for the stock. While passage has historically been unlikely, it remains the #1 risk to the multiple.
HIGH
A2A / Account-to-Account Payment Disruption Merchants are increasingly incentivising consumers to pay via bank account (bypassing the card rail entirely) to avoid 2–3% interchange fees. Open Banking regulations (Section 1033) make this easier. Visa's counter-strategy via Visa Direct (which also runs A2A) is the primary hedge.
MEDIUM
Global Macro / Consumer Softness A US recession would reduce payment volumes and cross-border travel. Sub-prime credit delinquencies reached 12.3% in mid-2025. While Visa doesn't hold credit risk directly (it earns a fee regardless of default), reduced spending volumes would compress revenue growth toward the low single digits.
MEDIUM
State-Backed Real-Time Payment Competition UPI (India), Pix (Brazil), and the US Federal Reserve's FedNow target emerging market and B2B flows where Visa is trying to expand. These government-subsidised rails charge zero or near-zero fees. Visa's response is to position itself as the cross-border layer above these domestic schemes.
LOW
FX Headwinds Visa guided to ~1–2% constant-currency headwind from a strong USD in current conditions. However, this is a translation effect, not a business fundamental — Visa's actual network volume is growing well above the headline rate in local currency terms.
LOW
Interchange Litigation Overhang The estimated interchange reimbursement fees at issue in unresolved U.S. claims stood at ~$39.4B as of October 2025 (down from $49.6B in 2023). Visa is actively settling and resolving cases. Non-GAAP results already exclude the impact, but GAAP results are periodically impacted by litigation provisions.

Overall: Visa's core business risk is low — it earns fees without holding credit risk, carries moderate debt, and generates >$22B in annual free cash flow. Primary risks are political and regulatory, not operational or competitive. This is the distinction that makes the valuation discount an opportunity rather than a warning.

08 — Conclusion

Investment Conclusion

Visa is one of the world's finest businesses — a capital-light, high-margin, recurring-revenue toll booth on global commerce. It earns money every time anyone, anywhere buys anything with a card. And it is currently priced as if the world is about to change that. It isn't — not materially, and not soon.
