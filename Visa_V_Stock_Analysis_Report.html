01 — Overview

Executive Summary

Visa enters mid-2026 trading at roughly $310, down ~12% year-to-date despite fundamentals that remain the envy of global financial services. The stock's 2026 drawdown is a narrative problem, not a numbers problem — reflecting three overlapping fears: the re-introduced Credit Card Competition Act, the DOJ's debit antitrust case, and stablecoin disintermediation risk.

Underneath, FY2025 delivered $40.0B revenue +11%, non-GAAP EPS $11.47 +14%, and 66% operating margins. This report forecasts Revenue per Share, EPS, Margins, ROIC and P/E for FY2026–FY2029 and compares each against public analyst consensus.

Note: Visa's fiscal year ends in September. FY2025 = October 2024–September 2025. FY2026 = October 2025–September 2026.

Why the Moat is So Durable

Visa is a textbook two-sided network: more merchants attract more cardholders, and vice versa. Once a payment network reaches Visa's scale — roughly 60% global card share outside China — the unit economics become nearly impossible to replicate. Every additional transaction costs Visa almost nothing incrementally, which is why operating margins have sat in the mid-60s for more than a decade.

Visa's infrastructure processes up to 65,000 messages per second with 99.999% uptime. Capital expenditure runs under 4% of revenue. Visa itself does not issue cards, does not extend credit, and does not take credit risk — it simply runs the rails and charges a toll on volume.

12 — Risks & Advantages

Structural Advantages & Key Risks

✅ Structural Advantages
  • Network effects at maximum scale. 4.6B cards, 150M+ merchants. Replication cost measured in decades.
  • Capital-light economics. ~4% of revenue in capex; ~57% FCF margin — extraordinary for a $40B revenue business.
  • Geographic diversification. International transactions ($14.2B) hedge against any single regulator's reach.
  • Stablecoin positioning is defensive. Visa is validating on Tempo, settling USDC/USDT, issuing stablecoin-linked cards in 100+ countries. It's absorbing the "threat."
  • Value-added services second engine. ~20% of revenue, compounding at 18–22%, with genuine pricing power independent of card volume.
  • Balance sheet. $13B cash, investment-grade ratings. Meaningful optionality for buybacks or strategic M&A.
  • AI leverage. Scale in transaction data makes Visa's fraud and dispute automation genuinely differentiated versus any new entrant.
⚠️ Primary Risks
  • Credit Card Competition Act (CCCA). Reintroduced January 2026. Would mandate routing alternatives, compressing interchange 5–15%. Probability of passage: ~20% — but impact is severe if it passes.
  • DOJ debit antitrust case. 2024 lawsuit heading toward a 2027 trial targeting Visa's debit tokenisation and client contracts.
  • EU/UK interchange caps. Ongoing pressure; EU cross-border interchange review pending 2026. Could compress international margins.
  • Stablecoin disintermediation risk. If stablecoins gain true retail product-market fit, cross-border margins could compress. Visa's position is hedged but imperfect.
  • Real-time payment systems. UPI (India), Pix (Brazil), FedNow (US) — domestic A2A rails that bypass card networks entirely. A genuine long-term emerging-market growth cap.
  • Travel / consumer slowdown. Cross-border is the highest-margin volume. A global tourism pullback hits EPS disproportionately.

13 — Context

Macro, Regulation & Competition

Macroeconomic Factors

  • Cash-to-digital tailwind. ~15% of global transactions still settled in cash — worth an estimated $4–5T in incremental annual volume by 2030. This is Visa's most durable secular driver.
  • Rates & multiples. A 50–100 bps Fed cutting cycle would re-expand Visa's P/E 2–3 turns — the single biggest factor in the bull case and the one macro factor that is actually in motion.
  • USD direction. Consensus expects a modestly weaker dollar in FY26–27, adding ~1–2% to reported revenue growth from international translation.
  • Inflation. Net positive for Visa (fees scale with nominal transaction values) up to ~6%. Above that level, consumer spending softness outweighs the nominal volume lift.

Regulatory Landscape

Base Case Regulatory Assumptions

  • CCCA does not pass (~80% probability given divided Congress dynamics).
  • DOJ case settles on behavioural remedies, not structural divestitures (~75% probability).
  • EU cross-border interchange revised down 2–5 bps in 2027 (~60% probability).
  • US GENIUS Act stablecoin framework remains constructive, enabling Visa's settlement strategy (~90% probability).

If any two of the first three go against Visa, the base case shifts to the bear scenario — roughly 10–15% lower price estimates.

Competitive Landscape

  • Mastercard. The "competitor" that moves in the same direction. The duopoly is stable — they barely compete on price. Visa is larger in debit; Mastercard slightly more exposed to VAS. Both face identical regulatory risks.
  • Stripe, Adyen, Block. Merchant-side gateways. They run on Visa rails most of the time. Competitive only at the acquiring layer Visa doesn't operate in. Partners, not threats.
  • PayPal, Apple Pay, Google Pay. Wallets, not networks. Almost all transactions still settle over Visa/Mastercard. They are distribution channels that actually expand Visa's addressable volume.
  • A2A real-time rails (UPI, Pix, FedNow). The most important long-term competitive vector. India's UPI processes volumes approaching Visa's global scale domestically. Government-backed, free-to-consumer, and explicitly designed to bypass card networks. Visa's biggest non-US structural risk over a 10-year horizon.
  • Stablecoins / on-chain. Visa is co-opting rather than competing — running validator nodes on Tempo, settling USDC, issuing stablecoin-linked cards. The threat is being absorbed into the network, which is exactly what Visa has done with every prior "disruptor."

14 — Conclusion

Investment Conclusion

Visa at $310 is priced for a world in which regulators win every fight, stablecoins kill the card, and value-added services never scale. The data suggests none of those are happening.

A buyer here is paying 23.9× forward earnings for a franchise with 30% ROIC, 66% operating margins, double-digit revenue growth, and a management team that returns virtually every dollar of free cash flow to shareholders.
